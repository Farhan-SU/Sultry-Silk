Karl Lagerfeld

There are few who cut as recognisable a silhouette as Karl Lagerfeld. His ponytailed silver hair tied, always, with a velvet ribbon, oversized sunglasses, high-collar shirts, Hedi Slimane suits and cut-off black leather driving gloves were as distinctive as personal aesthetics come. He’s one of the most prolific designer of the 20th and 21st centry, he is regarded as someone who defined contemporary luxury fashion industry and was known for his renaissance-like intelligence When presenting Mr.Lagerfiled with the Outstanding Achievement Award at the British Fashion Awards in 2015, Anna Wintour, editor of American Vogue, said:

“More than anyone I know, he represents the soul of fashion: restless, forward-looking and voraciously attentive to our changing culture.”

Karl Lagerfiled was a fashion polygot, held the creative director position of Fendi since 1965 making him the longest-standing creative director of a fashion house. His work at Fendi from 1967 saw him revitalise the leather goods brand.

His indelible presence in Fendi was ,however, just the begining as his greatest influence lies in his time as a creative director of Chanel from 1983 to present day. In 1982, he chairman of Chanel, Alain Wertheimer asked him to design for the house during a time when Chanel stared to loose its crowd because of their traditional style. But Mr.Lagerfiled saw this as a challenge and went on to ressurect the brand with his youthful designs while paying homage to the Chanel herself, subverting her ideals with his own modern take on her most renowned designs.

His great talent was his ability to identify, articulate, and frequently anticipate changes in the zeitgeist. Chanel had revolutionised fashion following the First World War by adapting traditional unstructured menswear shapes and fabrics (such as tweed and ticking) and repurposing them for womenswear.

“Lagerfeld’s drive, ambition and clarity of vision enabled him to take on not just multiple collections each season, but numerous fashion houses, defining each with his ability to combine a unique identity with an overriding sense of the present”, says The Courtauld Institute’s Rebecca Arnold.


Christian Dior

Christian Dior, French fashion reinvigorated the post-war Parisian fashion industry, with his extravagant use of fabric and flamboyant silhouettes which established the fashion empire as a symbol of class and grace. From 1937, Dior was employed by the fashion designer Robert Piguet, who gave him the opportunity to design for three Piguet collections. Dior would later say that

“Robert Piguet taught me the virtues of simplicity through which true elegance must come”. Blending simplicity with elegance, he created many signature looks of the 20th century that were loved by everyone from celebrities to Princess Diana and the Royal Family.

Dior’s iconic jacket, “Le Bar” and collection of swirling full skirts and nipped-in waists dubbed The New Look by Harper’s Bazaar became a post-war cultural symbol for what Dior himself described as ‘Youth, hope and the future.’ Immaculate tailoring and an emphasis on feminine curve with soft shoulders, full busts, and willowy waists would dominate women’s fashion for the next decade. Since Dior’s passing in 1957, fashion house has seen six successors from Yves Saint Laurent to Gianfranco Ferré, all of whom played an integral part in its enduring popularity.

Yves Saint Laurent

Finn Wolfhard for Saint Laurent F/W 2019 campaign by Anthony Vaccarello

Yves Saint Laurent paved the way for luxury ready-to-wear as the first contour house to do so. The contour house reflected the freedom and energy of the youth through their sensual yet trendy and sophisticated designs. Straight out of school at the age of 17, Christine Dior hired him on the spot in 1955 after seeing his drawings. In 1958, the 21 years old fashion prodigy exploded on the fashion scene as the world’s youngest couturier as the successor to Christian Dior, after Dior’s sudden death. His first dated sketches, which are held in the museum’s collection, go as far back as 1951 and depict sets and costumes for the theater and ballet. His legacy includes famous pieces such as Mondrian-inspired dresses, an Iris cardigan in homage to Van Gogh, safari and exotic peasant looks, and Le smoking. the Mondrian Dress features in the permanent collection of the Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York.

Saint Laurent’s was obsessed with idea of liberation and it reflected on his collections. From liberation of social norms of how women wore suits to liberation of couture and traditional couturier ideals. He learned the elegance of Dior’s style and adopted his own in his debut collection, spring 1958’s Trapeze line, which won him the Neiman Marcus prize. Saint Laurent created a lighter, more fluid silhouette with less fabric. This would be his first time transforming the contents of the modern woman’s wardrobe and then again in 1966 with the first tuxedo jacket for women in Le Smoking. Saint Laurent empowered them, dressing them to compete with men in trousers. Saint Laurent was influenced by the avant-garde style of artist Niki de Saint-Phalle, who reportedly often wore men’s suits with heels. Pierre Bergé, Saint Laurent’s long-time business partner, said

“If Chanel gave women their freedom, it was Saint Laurent who empowered them.”

He was the first to popularize this aesthesis for high fashion and proved to be the catalyst for people that embraced their individuality regardless of their sex or race in the 20th century. The trouser, Le Smoking, the Safari jacket are staples of the fashion lexicon ‘til this day


Miuccia Prada

Miuccia Prada is the co-chief executive officer and lead creative director of the Italian handbag and fashion label Prada. She inherited the business from her mother in 1978. The leather goods company—founded in 1913 by her maternal grandfather, Mario Prada, who had designed trunks for the Italian royal family. Three years later, with the encouragement of her husband Patrizio Bertelli, Prada debuted her womenswear line in 1988, which she described as “uniforms for the slightly disenfranchised.” She would add Miu Miu, a secondary line, in 1993, which now shows in Paris and has become a power brand in its own right.

In 1995, Prada released her first menswear line. Prada has received numerous accolades for her work, including the CFDA International Designer Award in 2004 and the first International Designer of the Year. In 2012, the Costume Institute at the Met dedicated the theme of their annual gala partially to Prada, themed “Schiaparelli And Prada: Impossible Conversations” examining the dialog between Prada and Elsa Schiaparelli as two iconic Italian women in fashion. At the 2013 British Fashion Awards. As of 2017, she is listed as the 79th most powerful woman in the world by Forbes. In 2018, the British Fashion Council awarded Miuccia Prada the Outstanding Achievement Award at The Fashion Awards 2018.

As a teenager and college student in Milanese vintage shops scouring for Yves Saint Laurent, and donned children’s clothes so as not to blend into the crowd, she perfected the skill of personal aesthetics. Consequently, her courage led her to revolutionized the fashion industry by bringing nylon to high fashion.

When Prada’s first nylon bags hit the runway in 1984, it was perceived as a radical approach to luxury and the antithesis of ’80s glamor. Speaking to Vogue, Miuccia said that her attraction to nylon came from the fact that she “hated all the bags that were around. They were so formal, so lady, so traditional, so classic.” Rebelling against stalwart codes of luxury fashion has become a Prada signature. By SS95, the brand was using nylon as an alternative to silk and cashmere across garments, and it became a hallmark of the brand ’til this day.
