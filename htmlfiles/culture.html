CULTURE

In a world of white noise and factional cacophony, a world where the first line of communication is visual, clothes are our shared language. The fabrics of our wardrobe is one of the way we express of ourselves and it can begin to become a part of our identity. The way one dresses and the ways they do constitutes expressions of degrees of social freedoms and influences. Lars Svendsen in his book ‘Fashion as a Philosophy, notes that fashion has “…conquered an increasing number of modern man’s fields of activity and has become almost ‘second nature’…” The logic of fashion he states, “…also encroaches on the areas of art, politics and science, [making it] clear that we are talking about a phenomenon that lies near the centre of the modern world.”

This type of freedom of expression ranges the full political gamut from conformity to rebellion. Dressing in a certain way that challenges societal norms spontaneously acquires political meaning. Rae Tutera, clothier and partner at Bindle & Keep, a bespoke suiting company based in New York City, working with the LGBTQ community to create suits for his trans clients that help them mirror on the outside who they are on the inside.

We all deserve to experience authentic and dignity, not just in our self-expression, but in all facets out our lives.

Rae Tutera for Posture Media

Fashion has always been used as a vehical for social change.

In the wake of the controversial reproductive right debate designers Prabal Gurung, for instance, ended his fashion show during 2017 New York Fashion Week with a line of models wearing shirts with a variety of political and activist phrases, including “Girls Just Want To Have Fundamental Rights” (based on the Cyndi Lauper song “Girls Just Want to Have Fun”) To Alessandro Michele with political statement pieces in his Gucci’s Resort 2020 collection advocating for pro-choice and basic human rights.

The initiative has so far funded more than 910,338 dollars in projects for reproductive and maternal health in countries ranging from Afganistan to Uganda.

From the purple jacquard pantsuit with “My body My Choice” in big block letters on the back to the mini capelet that accessorized a pajama top and matching skirt decorated with the date 22.05.1978, the day the Italian law protecting legal abortion took effect. Other pivotal looks included a pleated long sleeve gown with a uterus embroidered on the front to a T-shirt that read "Chime for Change"—the name of Gucci's gender equality campaign, which was launched in 2013. After the show, Michele said:

“I feel the need for freedom, at times I feel like I’m suffocating, and I need to launch messages through fashion. I have found freedom through fashion ever since I was a child, whenever it was necessary to speak up,” Michele said, after the show in Rome.

Michele, and by extension Gucci, are taking a clear stance against reproductive injustice here. This is the first time Michele has explicitly expressed his political stance on injustices but this not the first time that the brand has endorsed activism; a year ago, Gucci donated $500,000 to March for Our Lives, the gun control movement started following the school shooting in Parkland, Florida. .

"We stand with March for Our Lives and the fearless students across the country who demand that their lives and safety become a priority," Gucci told WWD. Michele also shared addtional support for the youg people speaking out about gun violence. "I am truly moved by the courage of these students," he said. "My love is with them and it will be next to them on March 24. I am standing with March for Our Lives and the strong young women and men across the United States who are fighting for their generation and those to come."
